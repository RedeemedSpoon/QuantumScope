Electronic Structure of Atomes

The electronic structure of atoms and molecules is a fundamental concept of quantum chemistry. It sheds light on the behavior of elements and allows us to understand the microscopic world around us. This structure is the basis of many physical and chemical properties of matter.

Nucleus and electrons: the main players

  • Nucleus: At the heart of the atom is the nucleus, dense and compact, made up of protons and neutrons. The number of protons, unique to each element, defines its atomic number (Z).
  • Electrons: Around the nucleus gravitate electrons, negatively charged particles and much lighter than protons and neutrons. These electrons do not rotate in circular orbits as was once thought, but occupy zones of probability of presence which are called atomic orbitals.

Organization of electrons: a set of layers and sublayers

  • Electronic layers: The electrons are distributed into electronic layers numbered 1 to 7, starting with the one closest to the nucleus (layer 1) and gradually moving away. Each shell can contain a maximum number of electrons given by the formula 2n², where n is the shell number.
  • Sublayers: Each electronic layer is subdivided into sublayers identified by letters (s, p, d, f). These subshells correspond to different shapes and energies of the atomic orbitals.

Filling of orbitals: the Aufbau principle and stability

  • Aufbau principle: Electrons prefer to occupy the lowest energy available orbitals first. This principle guides the filling of atomic orbitals.
  • Stability and electronic configuration: An atom is stable when its outermost orbitals are filled with electrons according to the Aufbau principle. The electronic configuration, which describes the distribution of electrons in orbitals, is a unique signature of each element and allows its chemical reactivity to be predicted.

Formation of molecules: a ballet of bonds

  • Chemical bonding: When two or more atoms bond to form a molecule, their atomic orbitals interact and combine to form molecular orbitals.
  • Types of molecular orbitals: Molecular orbitals can be bonding, antibonding or nonbonding. Bonding orbitals strengthen the bond between atoms, while antibonding orbitals weaken it. Non-bonding orbitals have no direct impact on bonding.
  • Molecular orbital theory (MOT): MOT is a powerful tool for explaining the formation and structure of chemical bonds in molecules. It allows us to understand the molecular geometry, reactivity and spectroscopic properties of molecules.
